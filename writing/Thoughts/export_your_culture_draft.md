# Export Your Culture

**Draft v4 | March 2026**

---

I was twelve when I first saw an Indian king in a video game.

Age of Empires II. The "Indians" civilization. War elephants draped in red and gold cloth, curved swords, the Delhi Sultanate campaign. I remember pausing the game to zoom in on the elephant units. Someone had cared enough to get the details right — the decorative howdah, the trunk raised in battle, the way the rider held his spear.

I had never felt so *seen* by a piece of media.

It was made by Ensemble Studios in Dallas, Texas.

That moment stayed with me not because of the game, but because of the absence it revealed. Where were the Indian studios making me feel that way? Where were the Indian stories traveling the world the way Japanese anime or American superhero films did?

Twenty-five years later, the answer is still mostly: nowhere. But for the first time, that might be changing.

---

## A Country's Image Is Written by Its Exports

In 1945, Japan was a defeated military empire. Atomic bombs had just fallen. The country was synonymous with war, imperialism, and generational trauma across Asia.

Eighty years later, Japan welcomed 42.7 million foreign tourists in 2025 — more visitors than the country has citizens under age 35. They spent $60 billion. Japan ranks 4th on the Global Soft Power Index. Four of the ten highest-grossing media franchises in human history are Japanese: Pokemon ($115 billion+), Hello Kitty, Mario, Anpanman. The Japanese government now targets ¥20 trillion — roughly $130 billion — in overseas content sales by 2033. Nearly double the value of all the cars Japan exports annually.

This didn't happen organically. Cool Japan was a $1.3 billion government investment in exporting anime, manga, and games as strategic instruments of national image. Japan's post-war constitution banned rebuilding conventional military power. So they rebuilt through stories instead. Three million foreign tourists visit anime and manga locations each year.

Korea ran the same playbook, harder. In the 1990s, South Korea was known for the Korean War, Samsung, and cheap manufacturing. Zero global cultural presence. Then the 1997 Asian financial crisis hit, and President Kim Dae-jung made a bet: cultural export as state priority. Ten trillion won in policy financing. Content business centers in fifty countries. A five-year plan to grow the K-culture market to 300 trillion won by 2030.

It worked. Korean content now accounts for 8% of all Netflix streams globally — second only to the United States. Squid Game debuted at #1 in all 93 countries where Netflix has top 10 lists — and drove a 76% surge in Korean language learners on Duolingo in the UK. A TV show made British teenagers want to learn Korean. That tells you everything about what cultural export actually produces — tourism pipelines, language demand, consumer brand affinity, talent immigration, geopolitical goodwill. All downstream of stories. This is the most durable compounding asset a country can build.

India barely has a position.

## India's Cultural Trade Deficit

India ranks 29th on the Global Soft Power Index. Japan is 4th.

India welcomed 9.95 million foreign tourists in 2024, excluding NRIs visiting family. Eleven times Japan's population, less than a quarter the visitors.

India owns zero of the top fifty highest-grossing media franchises globally. Japan owns four of the top ten — all born from stories it exported.

On Duolingo, Japanese is the 4th most studied language in the world. Korean is 6th — it recently surpassed Italian. Hindi doesn't crack the top ten. And *within India*, Korean language learning grew 75% year-over-year. Indian kids are learning Korean because K-dramas and BTS made them fall in love with Korean culture. No one outside India is learning Hindi because of Indian entertainment.

India imports stories at massive scale — Marvel, anime, K-pop, Netflix originals. The world imports approximately nothing from India. And the stories the world *does* have about India weren't written by Indians.

Steven Spielberg wrote one: *Indiana Jones and the Temple of Doom*. Monkey brains, human sacrifice, child slavery, a white American rescuing helpless villagers. The Indian government banned it. It grossed $333 million anyway. An entire generation's first "experience" of India was a filmmaker's fever dream of it.

The Simpsons wrote another: Apu Nahasapeemapetilon. For thirty years, the most prominent Indian character on American television was voiced by a white actor doing — as comedian Hari Kondabolu put it — "an impression of a white guy making fun of my father." Hank Azaria eventually apologized "to every single Indian person in this country." A generation of accent jokes had already been absorbed.

Danny Boyle wrote one more: *Slumdog Millionaire*. A British director makes a film about Indian poverty, wins eight Oscars, grosses $379 million. India's most globally visible film of the 2000s — and it's a slum story, told by an outsider looking in.

India in Western media is exotic-mystical-land or poverty-backdrop. There is no third option, because India hasn't written one.

India *does* export some culture. Bollywood has international box office. Diljit Dosanjh just grossed $101 million on a world tour. Pocket FM earns $100 million ARR from the United States. But look at who's consuming it. Diljit's sold-out stadiums in Vancouver and Toronto are filled overwhelmingly with Punjabi speakers who grew up with the culture. Bollywood's overseas numbers skew heavily toward NRI markets.

Compare that to BTS selling out arenas in São Paulo, Mexico City, and Berlin — to fans who don't speak a word of Korean. They learned Korean *because* of BTS.

The first is reach — your existing community finding you somewhere new. The second is gravity — strangers being pulled in. The cultural trade deficit only closes with gravity. Almost nothing India exports today has it.

India has the richest narrative tradition on earth — and it's structurally built for the franchise era.

The Mahabharata is 1.8 million words and 100,000 verses. But what makes it exportable IP isn't its age. It's its architecture. Morally grey protagonists — Arjuna frozen by doubt on the battlefield, Karna loyal to the wrong side out of honor, Duryodhana convinced he's the rightful heir. No single hero. No clean villain. Dynastic politics, betrayal, sibling warfare, and a divine charioteer who delivers philosophy while arrows fly. George R.R. Martin wrote three thousand pages of this and it became the biggest TV show in history. The Mahabharata did it three thousand years earlier, with more characters and deeper moral stakes.

The Ramayana predates most of the world's literary canons. The Panchatantra shaped fable traditions across Central Asia and Europe. The best animated Ramayana ever made — 9.1 on IMDb, ¥800 million budget — was produced by Yugo Sako, a Japanese Buddhist filmmaker, in 1992. He believed only animation could capture the epic's scale. He was right. He wasn't Indian.

Japan didn't export its oldest stories. It created Pokemon. Korea didn't export its oldest stories. It created K-dramas. But neither had source material this structurally rich. India's advantage is that the stories are built the way modern franchise IP needs to be built — ensemble casts, morally complex, infinitely expandable. The factory to turn them into products never existed. Until now.

## Closing the Deficit

Everyone in Indian entertainment talks about market size — 591 million gamers, 250 million short-form video users, $28 billion M&E sector. Those are consumption metrics. They measure how much India absorbs. Not what it sends out.

The conditions for export are different now. AI-native production collapsed costs — a single creator can produce a complete series from a laptop for under $20 a scene, where it used to take $300,000 and a twenty-person crew. Streaming eliminated distribution gatekeepers — RRR went from regional Telugu release to Netflix top 10 in 62 countries with no international strategy. And Black Myth: Wukong — a Chinese game steeped in Taoist tradition, zero Western concessions — crossed $1 billion on Steam, collapsing the risk premium on "too culturally specific." For investors, the question flipped: not "will non-Western stories travel?" but "which ones travel next?"

RRR is the clearest proof that gravity is possible. Nine consecutive weeks in the top non-English films on Netflix, Golden Globe for Best Original Song, standing ovation at the TCL Chinese Theatre from an American audience with zero connection to Telugu cinema. Craft and emotional intensity pulling in strangers on its own merit.

Games may be where the deeper opportunity sits. Film is passive — you watch a story. Games are interactive — you *inhabit* one. When Ghost of Tsushima made players feel Japanese culture so deeply that Sucker Punch, an American studio in Seattle, was named official tourism ambassador by the Japanese government, it proved something no Kurosawa film could: games create emotional ownership of a culture.

India has 15% of the world's gamers and generates less than 1.5% of global gaming revenue. The deeper gap: India consumes games. It doesn't create exportable ones. Gravity in games requires IP-first titles built for PC, PlayStation, and Steam — not mobile-casual.

Age of Bhaarat is the most interesting studio-level bet being placed right now. Its design philosophy matters more than its $60-70 million budget or Amitabh Bachchan co-founding it. They refuse to call the stories "myths." They call them "Indian epics." You don't play as Lord Ram. You play as a forest warden protecting the land from Rakshasa demons. Same universe, different entry point. Deeply rooted, never preachy. The same logic that made Black Myth: Wukong a billion-dollar hit.

I think about Age of Bhaarat and I'm twelve again, zooming in on those elephant units in Age of Empires, wondering why no Indian studio ever built the game I wanted to play. Thirty years later, one is trying.

DNEG's Ramayana is the highest-stakes binary bet in Indian entertainment. Directed by Nitesh Tiwari. Ranbir Kapoor as Rama, Yash as Ravana. Scored by Hans Zimmer and A.R. Rahman. Shot for IMAX. DNEG has won eight Academy Awards for Best Visual Effects. Part 1 releases Diwali 2026. If a Ramayana told by Indians, at world-class production quality, creates gravity with a global audience — it changes the math for every studio behind it. If it doesn't, the next $60 million bet on Indian IP becomes almost impossible to fund.

Pocket FM's US revenue — $100 million ARR, positioning as India's "new-age Disney" across 80+ markets — is the most interesting ambiguity in this piece. If those listeners are largely Indian-Americans, it's impressive reach. If non-Indians are discovering and paying for Indian audio stories, it's the first proof of serialized gravity. The answer matters more than the revenue number, and nobody is asking the question clearly.

India has capital. It has stories. It now has distribution. What it doesn't have is the craft layer — the talent pipelines, the writer-first culture, the game design programs, the narrative design training — that turns raw material into products with gravity. Volume without craft produces content graveyards. Korea didn't become a cultural superpower by producing eight hundred dramas. It became one by producing Squid Game.

Korea exports in Korean. Fifty-two million people, one language, one cultural pipeline, one brand. When a K-drama travels, it travels *as* Korean. When BTS sings, the language is the identity. Japan is the same.

India has twenty-two official languages and no single content pipeline. RRR is Telugu. Sacred Games is Hindi. Diljit sings in Punjabi. Pocket FM produces across fourteen languages. Hindi alienates half the country — try telling someone in Chennai that Hindi is "the Indian language." English is the global export language, but most Indians don't speak it well enough to produce native-quality English content at scale, and English-language content doesn't build *Indian* cultural identity the way Korean-language content builds Korean identity. You can't K-pop your way to gravity when there's no single "K" to rally around.

India may never have a unified cultural export brand. The question is whether it needs one — or whether it exports in multiple streams, each finding different global niches. Telugu cinema for action spectacle. Punjabi music for diaspora-to-crossover. Hindi serialized content for South Asian narrative traditions. English-language gaming for global platforms. Streaming and AI dubbing are collapsing the language barrier for distribution — RRR traveled in 20+ languages without a Hindi intermediary. But distribution isn't identity. Korea knows what "Korean culture" means globally. India doesn't have that coherence, and building it may be the hardest part of closing the deficit — harder than studios, talent, or funding.

India also has no coordinated strategy for any of this. Korea has run state-backed cultural export since 1998 — ten trillion won, content centers in fifty countries, five-year plans. Japan since 2013 with Cool Japan. India held its first media and entertainment summit in 2025 — WAVES, the Prime Minister invoking an "Orange Economy." A summit is not a strategy. India needs a dedicated cultural export fund, co-production treaties, tax incentives for studios building global IP. None of this exists. And the domestic market can't justify the budgets alone — AAA game development costs $60-70 million, high-production animation isn't cheap. Every serious Indian content studio needs to be built with global revenue as the primary target.

---

India's image will be rewritten in the next decade. The economics make it possible for the first time — production costs collapsing, distribution global on day one, capital institutional rather than shoestring. The stories have always been here.

The cultural trade deficit closes with gravity — a stranger pausing to zoom in on a detail an Indian studio got right, wanting to know more about a culture they never knew they needed.

Japan built that gravity. Korea built that gravity. Thirty years after a studio in Dallas made a twelve-year-old in India feel seen, Indian studios are trying to build their own.
